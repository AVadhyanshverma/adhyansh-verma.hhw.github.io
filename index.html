01 Introduction to Writer

What is LibreOffice Writer?

LibreOffice Writer is a powerful, free, and open-source word processing software that serves as the core text-editing component of the LibreOffice suite. Born from the legacy of OpenOffice.org in 2010 when The Document Foundation forked the project, Writer is designed to be a fully featured alternative to commercial software like Microsoft Word. At its heart, a word processor is a digital environment where users can create, edit, format, store, and print text-based documents—but Writer's capabilities stretch far beyond simple typing.

Writer provides a robust architecture for document creation, allowing users to build everything from a simple one-page memo to a complex, multi-chapter book complete with diagrams, indexes, and automated cross-references. Because it is open-source, it is continuously improved by a global community of thousands of developers, ensuring it remains updated with modern file formats, including seamless compatibility with .docx, .rtf, and .pdf files. Writer uses the Open Document Format (ODF), an ISO-certified international standard, as its native file format—ensuring your documents remain accessible and readable for decades to come, free from vendor lock-in.

💡 Did You Know?

LibreOffice is used by over 200 million people worldwide and is the default office suite installed on millions of government, educational, and business computers across Europe, South America, and Asia. Countries like Italy, France, and Brazil have mandated its use in public offices to reduce software licensing costs.

A Brief History of Word Processing

Before digital word processors, documents were created on typewriters—machines that offered no ability to edit, undo, or reformat. The first true word processing software emerged in the late 1970s with programs like WordStar, which allowed text to be edited on a screen before printing. In the 1980s, WordPerfect dominated, followed by Microsoft Word which gained market dominance through its integration with Windows. The open-source movement of the 2000s gave birth to OpenOffice, which eventually evolved into LibreOffice in 2010. Today, Writer represents decades of evolution in document creation technology—combining the reliability of traditional word processing with the flexibility of modern, community-driven software development.

Key Features of Writer

Intelligent Spell Check & AutoCorrect

Writer doesn't just highlight misspelled words; it actively corrects common typographical errors on the fly. It utilizes comprehensive, customizable dictionaries supporting over 100 languages and integrated grammar checking powered by LanguageTool to ensure linguistic accuracy across multilingual documents.

Extensive Formatting Tools

Users have granular control over typography and layout—manipulating font families, adjusting line spacing to the millimeter, applying custom color palettes, managing kerning and character spacing, and utilizing complex paragraph alignments including hanging indents and drop caps to craft visually striking, publication-ready documents.

Find & Replace with Regular Expressions

Beyond finding a specific word, Writer's advanced search allows users to locate text based on specific formatting attributes or use "regular expressions" (regex)—a pattern-matching language—to find complex text patterns. For example, you could find every date in a document matching the format "DD/MM/YYYY" and reformat them all at once, making document-wide edits incredibly fast and precise.

Mail Merge & Native PDF Export

Mail Merge connects a document to a database or spreadsheet and auto-generates personalized copies for every individual—essential for sending hundreds of customized letters, certificates, or invoices. The native PDF exporter supports "Hybrid PDFs"—files that look standard to any PDF reader yet remain fully editable when reopened in Writer, bridging the gap between sharing and editing.

Master Documents & Navigator

For large-scale projects like books or theses, Writer's Master Document feature allows you to split work across multiple files while treating them as a single document for purposes of page numbering, indexing, and cross-referencing. The Navigator panel provides a bird's-eye view of your document structure, letting you jump instantly to any heading, table, image, or bookmark.

Uses of Writer in Daily Life

For students, Writer is the primary tool for drafting essays, compiling research data, formatting project files, and preparing examination notes with structured headings and bullet points. In professional environments, offices rely on it to generate formal correspondence, draft business proposals, create invoices, and publish company newsletters with consistent branding. Creative writers and journalists use it for drafting manuscripts, utilizing chapter organization, word-count tracking, and version history to manage large-scale creative projects with ease. Even government organizations worldwide use Writer for official documentation, leveraging its ODF format compliance for long-term archival and interoperability.

📌 Key Takeaways
  • Writer is a free, open-source word processor used by millions globally
  • It supports all major file formats including .docx, .rtf, and native PDF export
  • Features like regex search, mail merge, and master documents make it professional-grade
  • The ODF format ensures long-term document accessibility and freedom from vendor lock-in

02 Introduction to Styles

What are Styles in Writer?

To truly master LibreOffice Writer, one must understand the concept of "Styles." Most beginners format their text manually—highlighting a word, clicking the "Bold" button, and changing the font size to 16pt to create a heading. This is known as direct formatting. A "Style," on the other hand, is a centralized, predefined set of formatting rules saved under a specific name, such as "Heading 1" or "Quotation." Think of it like a CSS stylesheet for a web page—just as web developers separate HTML content from CSS presentation, Writer separates your document's content from its visual presentation.

Applying a style is like applying a master blueprint to your text. Instead of manually applying five different formatting changes to create a heading, you simply apply the "Heading 1" style, and the text instantly adopts all the predefined characteristics—font family, size, weight, color, spacing, and even the space before and after the paragraph. This separation of content from presentation is a principle highly valued in modern digital design and is the foundation of professional document creation.

Types of Styles

a. Paragraph Styles

The most commonly used type. Governs the complete layout of a paragraph—font family, text size, line spacing, text alignment (left, center, justified), background color, indentation, and the space above and below. Every paragraph in Writer has a paragraph style, even if it's just "Default Paragraph Style."

b. Character Styles

Applied to a specific range of text within a paragraph without affecting the rest. Perfect for consistently highlighting technical terms, foreign words, or emphasis. For example, an "Important Term" style could apply bold + dark red color to any selected text.

c. Page Styles

Dictate the physical page layout—dimensions (A4, Letter, Legal), margins, headers, footers, page numbering, columns, and background watermarks. A single document can chain multiple page styles—e.g., "First Page" (no header) followed by "Default" (with running headers).

d. Frame & List Styles

Frame styles format elements like text boxes and images, controlling borders, background fills, and text wrap behavior. List styles control bulleted or numbered list hierarchy—specifying bullet symbols, numbering schemes (1, a, i, I), and indentation levels.

How to Apply and Modify Styles

1
Open the Styles Deck: Press F11 or navigate to View › Styles. A sidebar panel opens showing all available styles organized by category.
2
Apply a Style: Place your cursor in the target paragraph, then double-click the desired style name (e.g., "Heading 1"). The text instantly transforms to match all the style's formatting rules.
3
Modify a Style: Right-click any style name and select "Modify Style." A dialog opens where you can change font, spacing, borders, indentation, and more. Click OK—every paragraph using that style updates immediately throughout the document.
4
Create a Custom Style: Format a paragraph exactly how you want it. Then open the Styles Deck, click the "New Style from Selection" button, give it a descriptive name, and your custom style is saved for reuse anywhere in the document.

Advantages of Using Styles

Absolute Consistency

When writing a 50-page report, ensuring every single subheading looks exactly identical is nearly impossible if done manually. Styles guarantee 100% consistency across the entire document—every "Heading 2" will have the exact same font, size, color, and spacing, no matter how many you create.

Rapid Global Modifications

Imagine finishing a project and realizing all 30 subheadings need to change from blue to green, and from 14pt to 16pt. With direct formatting, you'd have to find and change each one manually—a tedious, error-prone process. With styles, you simply edit the "Heading 2" style definition once, and Writer instantly updates all 30 headings simultaneously in milliseconds.

Foundation for Automation

Many of Writer's advanced features rely entirely on styles. You cannot automatically generate a Table of Contents without using Paragraph Styles to define your heading hierarchy. Cross-references, outline numbering, and document indexes all depend on properly applied styles—they are the backbone of intelligent document automation.

💡 Did You Know?

Professional publishers and typographers always use styles-based workflows. Major organizations like the European Parliament and NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory use LibreOffice styles to maintain consistent formatting across thousands of official documents produced every year.

03 Working with Images

How to Insert Images in Writer

A text-heavy document can quickly become monotonous and difficult to navigate; images are essential for illustrating concepts, providing visual evidence, and breaking up visual fatigue. Writer offers multiple pathways to bring graphics into your workspace. The standard method is navigating to Insert › Image, which opens a file browser to locate pictures on your hard drive. However, modern workflows support faster methods: you can drag an image file directly from a folder onto the Writer canvas, or copy an image from a web browser and paste it straight into the document using Ctrl+V.

Writer supports a comprehensive array of image formats including JPG (photographs), PNG (graphics with transparency), SVG (scalable vector graphics that never pixelate), GIF (simple animations), BMP (uncompressed bitmaps), TIFF (high-quality print images), and even WebP (modern web-optimized format). When inserting images, Writer gives you the choice to either embed the image data directly into the document file or link to the external file—embedding makes the document self-contained and portable, while linking keeps the file size small but requires the original image to remain accessible.

Resizing and Moving Images

Inserting an image is rarely the final step; it almost always needs spatial adjustment to fit harmoniously within your page layout. When you click on an inserted image, a bounding box appears with eight small squares, known as "sizing handles," located on the corners and midpoints of each edge.

Proportional Resizing

Dragging an edge handle stretches the image in one direction, often distorting it. To maintain the original aspect ratio (the proportional relationship between width and height), hold Shift while dragging a corner handle. This scales the image perfectly without making it look squished or stretched.

Precise Dimensions

For exact sizing, right-click the image and select Properties › Type tab. Here you can enter precise width and height values in centimeters or inches, and lock the aspect ratio with a checkbox to ensure proportional scaling.

Moving & Anchoring

Images can be anchored To a Paragraph (moves with text flow), To a Character (precise inline positioning), To the Page (stays locked to a physical coordinate), or As Character (treated like a large text character within a line).

Positioning Guides

Writer displays alignment guides when dragging images near the center or edges of the page, helping you position images with precision. You can also use the Position and Size dialog (F4) for pixel-perfect placement.

Text Wrapping Options

Text wrapping is the engine that dictates how your paragraphs interact with your images. This is what separates an amateur-looking document from a professionally laid-out one. Accessing these settings via right-click › Wrap provides several layout options:

None (No Wrap)

The image acts like a massive wall. Text stops above the image and resumes below it, leaving wide, empty white space on either side. Useful for full-width images or diagrams that need to stand alone.

Parallel

Text flows naturally around all bounding sides of the image, framing it symmetrically. This is the most common wrapping mode for in-text illustrations and is the standard in magazine and newspaper layouts.

Optimal

Writer intelligently calculates which side of the image has the widest gap and wraps text only on that side, preventing text from being squeezed into tiny, hard-to-read columns. Best for images placed near page margins.

Through

The image floats completely independent of text flow. Text passes directly behind or in front of the image—ideal for creating watermarks, decorative backgrounds, or layered visual effects in creative documents.

Formatting Images (Borders, Alignment, Filters)

To make images look integrated and professional, Writer offers a comprehensive Image Properties dialog (accessible via double-click or right-click › Properties). Here, you can apply borders of varying thickness, styles (dashed, dotted, solid, double-line), and colors to frame the picture elegantly. You can manage internal margins—the space between the border and the image itself—ensuring breathing room between your picture and surrounding text.

Furthermore, Writer includes a built-in image toolbar that appears whenever a picture is selected, allowing for on-the-fly adjustments like altering brightness, contrast, color saturation, and gamma values. You can apply visual filters including grayscale (converting color photos to black-and-white), charcoal sketch (an artistic pencil-drawing effect), and posterize (reducing color depth for a pop-art look)—all directly within the word processor without needing a separate photo-editing application like Photoshop or GIMP.

📌 Key Takeaways
  • Writer supports JPG, PNG, SVG, GIF, TIFF, WebP, and more image formats
  • Always hold Shift when resizing to maintain aspect ratio
  • Image anchoring determines whether pictures move with text or stay fixed on the page
  • Text wrapping controls are essential for professional-looking document layouts
  • Built-in filters eliminate the need for external image editing software for basic adjustments

04 Advanced Features of Writer

Table of Contents (TOC)

The automated Table of Contents is arguably one of the most powerful academic features in Writer. Manually typing an index and trying to match page numbers is frustrating and error-prone, especially because page numbers shift every time you add or remove content. Writer automates this entirely—it scans the document, reading the hierarchy of your Paragraph Styles (Heading 1 for main chapters, Heading 2 for subtopics, Heading 3 for sub-subtopics, and so on). By navigating to Insert › Table of Contents and Index, the software instantly generates a neatly formatted, clickable index that mirrors your document's structure.

Auto-Update Feature

If you add three pages of new information to Chapter 1, pushing Chapter 2 further down the document, you don't need to manually recalculate anything. Simply right-click the TOC and select "Update Index." Writer instantly refreshes all page numbers perfectly—a task that would otherwise take hours of painstaking manual correction in a long document.

Customization Options

The TOC is fully customizable—you can choose which heading levels to include (e.g., only Heading 1 and 2), add tab leaders (the dotted lines connecting titles to page numbers), change the font and spacing of each level independently, and even add the TOC title in your preferred style. This level of control ensures your index matches the exact formatting requirements of any academic or professional standard.

Templates

A Template is essentially a master blueprint for future documents. Saved with an .ott file extension instead of the standard .odt, a template stores your preferred styles, page margins, fonts, headers, footers, and even pre-written placeholder text. For instance, if your school requires all assignments to have a specific cover page layout, specific font sizes, 1.5× line spacing, and a particular header format, you can configure this setup once and save it as a template.

The next time you need to write an assignment, you open the template. Writer creates a brand-new, unnamed document with all your settings pre-loaded—you simply start typing your content without spending time on repetitive formatting setup. Organizations use templates extensively to maintain brand consistency; every letter, invoice, and report follows the exact same visual identity automatically.

💡 Did You Know?

LibreOffice comes with dozens of pre-built templates for resumes, business letters, presentations, and academic papers. You can also download hundreds more from the official LibreOffice Extensions website. Many schools and universities provide their own custom templates with pre-configured formatting that students can import directly into Writer.

Track Changes

In the real world, documents are rarely written by just one person; they are reviewed, edited, and revised collaboratively—sometimes by dozens of contributors. Track Changes is the system that manages this collaboration transparently. When activated via Edit › Track Changes › Record, Writer begins monitoring every keystroke. If a reviewer deletes a word, it doesn't disappear—it gets crossed out in a distinctive color. If they add a sentence, it appears underlined in a different color. Each reviewer is automatically assigned a unique color, making it easy to distinguish who made which edit.

Accept or Reject Changes

Reviewers can highlight text and leave floating comment bubbles in the margins explaining why a change is needed—providing context that simple deletions cannot convey. Once the document is returned to the original author, they can go through the edits one by one using the Track Changes toolbar, right-clicking to "Accept Change" (making it permanent) or "Reject Change" (reverting to the original text), giving complete control over the final draft.
